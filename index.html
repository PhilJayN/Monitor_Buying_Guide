Primary Use

One can walk into an electronics store, close one's eyes, purchase a monitor, and be extremly happy, or extremely disssapointed. Therefore it's important to purchase a monitor based on your needs. There are monitors for gamers, graphic designers, programmers and analysts, and everyday use. Picking a monitor based on what you need it to do will generally result better results.

Pick Price Range

It's good to have a budget and add about $50 to $100 to that budget. A monitor is something you'll mostly likely spend 8 hours a day on. Furthermore, many people keep monitors for years. Typically a monitor will see about 2-10 years of its life with the same owner. Try not to divorce your monitor. Returning a monitor is not as easy: it's heavy, bulky, costly to ship, and many stores aren't going to accept a monitor return with open arms. So make sure you don't go too cheap on a monitor.

Pick a Size

Size is a personal preference, and frankly very subjective. But some thought should go into it. 23" - 27" is the most common. Remember! Screen size and pixel should be picked deliberately, not haphazardly. You want screen size and resolution to mesh with each other nicely, like ketchup and french fries.

Most people would agree that a 23" with 4k resolution is not a good match. There's too many pixels on a small screen size, so text would be too small and difficult to read. On the other hand, 32" with a 1920 x 1080 pixel would be very pixelated. Text by default will look too big, bulky, and pixelated. Interestly, some people are very happy because the bigger text will be easier to read.

The sweet spot for monitor size, though, is 27" at 2560 x 1440.

Screen Real Estate

Note that "screen real estate" refers to the amount of virtual space available for the user to work with, more space to have more windows and applications all opened all at the same time, side by side. A 27" monitor at 2560 x 1440 will provide more space to work with than a 27" monitor at 1920 x 1080.

Pick Eye Care Tech

Do you suffer from migrane, headaches, or eyestrain while using the computer? If so, then getting a monitor with flicker free technology will be beneficial.

Typically, each company has their own names for this technology, but its function remains the same. Be on the lookout for naming:

  • Acer - Flicker-less
  • Asus - Eye care
  • LG - Flicker safe
  • BenQ - ZeroFlicker or Flicker-free

Warranty and Customer Service

Customer service is one of those things that one doesn't really need, until one really needs it. Most monitor purchased are usually defect free, with the exception of cases where the consumer will get monitors with ghosting, dead pixels, stuck pixels, ghosting, etc... Because of this, it's important to consider how pain-free or pain-ful a company's RMA (return) process is. Consider each company's reputation and the number of warranty years they offer for their products. Typically, warranty varies from 1 year - 3 years.
