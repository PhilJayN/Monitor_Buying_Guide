It aims to be as consumer-centric as possible, without bogging down with too much detail. Instead, it will focus on the broader picture: why a consumer would need one panel vs another panel.

Primary Use

One can walk into an electronics store, close one's eyes, purchase a monitor, and be extremly happy, or extremely disssapointed. Therefore it's important to purchase a monitor based on your needs. There are monitors for gamers, graphic designers, programmers and analysts, and everyday use. Picking a monitor based on what you need it to do will generally result better results.

Warning: The choice you pick here will determine what options show up on the following page. For example, if you choose your primary use to be productivity and you never play a single game, then the monitor options for gaming will not be discusssed because, well, you won't need it.

Pick Price Range

It's good to have a budget and add about $50 to $100 to that budget. A monitor is something you'll mostly likely spend 8 hours a day on. Furthermore, many people keep monitors for years. Typically a monitor will spend about 2-10 years of its life with the same owner. Try not to divorce your monitor. Returning a monitor is not as easy: it's heavy, bulky, costly to ship, and many stores aren't going to accept a monitor return with open arms. So make sure you don't go too cheap on a monitor.

Pick Aspect Ratio

Note: I've decided to discuss aspect ratio first, and then and we' ll talk about monitor size. It makes more sense to decide on an aspect ratio first because you'll see soon that it determines the size. Consider this lopsided scenario: You first decide on a 23" for a size. Then you find out that 23" doesn't come in an aspect ratio of 21:9. This is why I've decided to discuss aspect ratios first.

While monitor shoppping, you'll come into terms like aspect ratios, and you'll see numbers like 16:9, 16:10, 21:9, etc... Rather than getting a headache about this, decide what you need the monitor for. Many movies support 21:9 aspect ratio, so getting an ultrawide with a 21:9 aspect ratio is great. The aspect ratio 16:9 is quite common in mainstream stores. You'll find the majority of monitors in modern electronics store to fall into this range.

21:9 (think ultrawide) aspect ratio is great for the following, provided that the games or the particular movie you are watching actually supports it:

  • Watching movies
  • Playing games
  • Content creation, movie editing, programming, excel

16:9 aspect ratio is a great all rounder, jack of all trades

Pick a Size

Size is a personal preference, and frankly very subjective. But some thought should go into it. 23" - 27" is the most common. Remember! Screen size and pixel should be picked deliberately, not haphazardly. You want screen size and resolution to mesh with each other nicely, like ketchup and french fries.

Most people would agree that a 23" with 4k resolution is not a good match. There's too many pixels on a small screen size, so text would be too small and difficult to read. On the other hand, 32" with a 1920 x 1080 pixel would be very pixelated. Text by default will look too big, bulky, and pixelated. Interestly, some people are very happy because the bigger text will be easier to read.

The sweet spot for monitor size, though, is 27" at 2560 x 1440.

Screen Real Estate

Note that "screen real estate" refers to the amount of virtual space available for the user to work with, more space to have more windows and applications all opened all at the same time, side by side. A 27" monitor at 2560 x 1440 will provide more space to work with than a 27" monitor at 1920 x 1080.

Pick a Panel

IPS (In-Plane Switching)

You most likely have an IPS right next to you! Check your phone. More often than not, it is equipped with an IPS panel because phone screens need to look great, regardless how the consumer holds it. IPS panels can be described as "vibrant", "colorful", and "lively", with great viewing angles, and thus less color shift. To better demonstrate viewing angle, take your phone screen and move its screen in different direction. You'll notice that the colors of the picture will change very little, even at different angle. This is the power of an IPS panel.

Some people like IPS because it allows them to lay down on the ground and exercise (do situp, pushups) while watching a movie. IPS panel's viewing angle also allow two or more people to sit next to a screen and watch a movie together, or work on project together: Both people will be able to see the same color and clarity. Due to the amazing viewing angles, the colors remain consistent. Try doing this with TN panels, and you'll discover that the pictures will have yellow to it, or look completely washed out.

Pick Finish

Screen Finish

There is a misconception that people have about IPS panels being "glossy", or "too shiny". This is WRONG and likely comes from the fact that phones and tablet (which typically have IPS panels) are by nature very glossy, shiny, and reflective. The panel type has nothing do with how reflective or non-reflective a screen looks. Rather, it's just a matter of the type of coating a manufacturer decides to put onto the monitor.

A monitor being matte or glossy is a matter of personal preference (and in some cases, color reproduction). Back in the 1990s, companies sold matte screens to put over monitors to give the user some privacy and make the monitors less shiny. Nowadays, monitor technology is so good that even matte screen look amazing compared to their glossy counterpart. Some people must have a matte monitor at all cost, while some just don't care at all.

For those who care, this section is perfect for you.

Matte monitors offer the following advantages:

  • Less annoyance due to less reflection
  • Less eyestrain (subjectively)
  • Untainted picture quality because outside objects aren't reflected into monitor
  • Cannot see people's reflection sneaking behind you

How to Tell if Monitor's Screen is Glossy or Matte

Typically, the product specifications will use the following keywords:

  • Anti-glare
  • Anti-glare coating
  • Matte finish, matte screen

If you are unable to find this information, consider looking at the monitor's manuals, which you can typically find by typing "[monitor model name] manual" into Google. Dell.com has an amazing search engine for their monitors. If you still cannot find the information, consider going to Reddit's many subreddits, such as /r/monitors, /r/pcmasterrace, /r/ultrawidemasterrace and asking there. Just make sure to do some research and mention that you've tried looking for specs and manuals but couldn't find it. You'll increase your chances of getting help if you make some sort of attempt to research first.

Pick Eye Care Tech

Do you suffer from migrane, headaches, or eyestrain while using the computer? If so, then getting a monitor with flicker free technology will be beneficial.

Typically, each company has their own names for this technology, but its function remains the same. Be on the lookout for naming:

  • Acer - Flicker-less
  • Asus - Eye care
  • LG - Flicker safe
  • BenQ - ZeroFlicker or Flicker-free

Pick Mounting Option and Vesa Support

If you do not have any plans on mounting your monitor on a stand, and would like to use the default stand that comes with the monitor, then there is no need to consider whether a monitor comes with Vesa mounts. If you're opting for the default monitor stand, consider the following:
  • Height adjust - For ergonomic reasons, you want to be able to adjust the monitor up and down as you please
  • Pivot and tilt - A nice stand should offer these, if you decide you want movement freedom
  • Landscape and portrait mode - Some people prefer to orient their monitors in a portrait position to allow them to have more vertical space.

Vesa Mounting

If you're unsure whether you want to use the stand or Vesa mount in the future, then spend the extra money now to make sure you have Vesa mounting option, should you ever decide you want it. You'll find yourself in a predicament should you want to use Vesa mount in the future and your monitor lacks it. However, there adapters to use for monitors without Vesa mount holes, but a monitor with built in Vesa mount holes will give you less headaches.

Pick X-Sync Technology: G-sync or Freesync

Lets talk about $$$ upfront

These two techs will add more cost to a monitor, with G-sync typically costing more ($100 - $200) than AMD's Freesync. If you have a budget, get Freesync. Note: If you want to utilize a monitor with Freesync, you need a AMD GPU. Similarily, if you want to utilize a monitor with G-sync, you'll need an Nvidia GPU.

Nvidia's G-sync and AMD's Freesync is another debate and discussion that could takes hours or days, and the differences and similarities between these two tech will takes pages of write-up. So to keep it simple, just keep in mind that G-sync and Freesync will give gamers a "smooth, buttery feeling. Some would consider G-sync and Freesync to be a luxury, while others consider these two technology a must have. But for all intents and purposes, for the average consumer, G-sync and Freesync will be a luxury item because it adds to the price of the monitor.

It is important to note that if a user has never experienced G-sync for Freesync, then the user will not miss out on anything. But the moment a user has experienced using these two techs to play games, it is very difficult to play games without these techs. Going back is very difficult.

Warranty and Customer Service

Warning: What I'm about to say next is very opinionated, but I hope that you'll consider it while making a purchase decision: When buying monitor, consider both the seller and the manufacturer's reputation and customer service. Customer service is one of those things that one doesn't really need, until one really needs it. Most monitor purchased are usually defect free, with the exception of cases where the consumer will get monitors with ghosting, dead pixels, stuck pixels, ghosting, etc... Because of this, it's important to consider how pain-free or pain-ful a company's RMA (return) process is. It's not a good feeling to be on the receiving end of bad luck with a poor quality monitor and have to deal with terrible customer service. You'll end up talking to the bad seller, who will advise you to contact the manfucturer, who has a headache inducing customer service department who will make you ride a roller coaster to get your monitor fixed. At this point, you'll wish that you had spent the extra dollars and purchased from a reputable seller and manufacturer. Also, consider each company's reputation and the number of warranty years they offer for their products. Typically, warranty varies from 1 year - 3 years.

Pick a Refresh Rate

I'm going to keep it simple with refresh rates: Get a monitor with the highest refresh rate that you can afford AND that your GPU can handle. Typically monitors with high refresh rates will cost more.

The average mainstream monitor comes with refresh rate of 60hz, so you'll get your standard 60fps gameplay. This is enough for most people, and it will give a very good gaming experience. If you decide to go for monitors with much higher refresh rates, make sure to do more research and look at benchmarks to be sure that your current GPU can actually utlize high refresh rates.

So what happens if you decide to get a monitor with a high refresh rate but your GPU cannot put out enough frames per second? Lets say you get a monitor with 144hz, but your GPU can only push out 60fps on a game because it's not powerful enough? Well, nothing bad happens, but you just won't be able to utulize all that high refresh rate goodness. You can always upgrade your GPU later on.

One thing to keep in mind is that if a user has never expeienced higher refresh rates, they won't miss anything. Life goes on as normal. But if a user has been playing on a 144hz monitor, and they have to go back to a 60hz monitor, they will feel the pain because game play will not feel buttery smooth anymore. It's the same situation with x-sync technology: If you've never used it, don't worry, you're not missing out. But if you use it, you cannot go back.
